How the autopsy works

The point of this project is not to claim the machine wins. It is to show, honestly, what the numbers could and could not say.

01 · Point in time

Each company is scored on the last annual report filed before the fraud was public. No hindsight in the inputs. Hindsight is only in knowing where to look, and that bias is the whole conversation below.

02 · A transparent rubric, not a black box

The red-flag score is a deterministic model, not an opaque number an AI made up. Every point traces to a filing figure: accruals, cash conversion, revenue growth, capital-spend versus cash, receivables, leverage. You can see exactly what fired and why.

03 · Real filings, cited

Controls come straight from SEC XBRL. The pre-XBRL frauds (Enron, WorldCom) are transcribed from the actual 10-K and every figure links to it. Wirecard is a foreign filer shown with that gap stated out loud, not hidden.

04 · Where Claude comes in

The quantitative score is arithmetic. Claude Code did the forensic reading around it: pulling filings, encoding the signals, and writing the point-in-time memo on each case from the footnotes, labelled as the qualitative read it is.

05 · Scored against reality

Ground truth is known: three of these were frauds that collapsed, one more too, and four are still-running honest companies. The detector's verdict is graded into a real confusion matrix, true and false, hit and miss.

06 · Honest by construction

The controls include a company the detector wrongly flags, and the frauds include one it misses entirely. Those two cells are not failures of the demo. They are the finding.

What this cannot tell you

  • Hindsight is baked into the question. I already knew which companies were frauds, so I knew where to point the model. Real-time, blind, across thousands of filers, the false-positive rate would be far higher than it looks here.
  • A flag is a question, not a verdict. Netflix is flagged and is a perfectly honest company. Aggressive-looking accounting and fraud can produce the same ratios. The tool raises a hand; it does not convict.
  • The best frauds are invisible to the filings. Wirecard's numbers looked pristine because the lie was cash that did not exist. You cannot catch a missing asset by reading the assets that were reported.
  • Small sample, curated. Nine companies is a teaching set, not a validated screen. The thresholds were chosen to be reasonable, not optimized, and the matrix would move on a bigger, blinder universe.
  • Filings lag and are self-reported. Annual reports arrive months after year-end and describe what management chose to disclose. This is a read of the paper trail, not the ground truth.
